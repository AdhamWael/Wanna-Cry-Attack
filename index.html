Attack Analysis

1. What happened and who was behind the attack?

On 12 May 2017, the WannaCry ransomware cryptoworm launched a global cyberattack. The malware attacked Microsoft Windows-powered computers and encrypted data on them before demanding $300-$600 worth of Bitcoin to be paid in ransom. It was unparalleled in size, with more than 150 nations and over 300,000 computers falling prey to it. Unlike regular ransomware, it contained an automatically propagated "worm" component that caused it to automatically move from system to system through networks.

U.S and U.K did not make an official determination regarding the attribution until December 2017, by which time they identified the North Korean state-sponsored cyber group Lazarus Group as the attacker. North Korea has denied involvement.

2. How did the hackers manage to break in?

The success of WannaCry relied on an effective exploit of an acknowledged, albeit frequently unpatched, Window vulnerability:

  • The Exploit (EternalBlue): The exploit that the attackers exploited was called "EternalBlue," an NSA-made cyberweapon. The exploit was recently stolen and released by a group known as The Shadow Brokers a month before.
  • The Vulnerability (MS17-010): EEternalBlue exploited a vulnerability in the Server Message Block (SMB) protocol used by Microsoft. Although in March 2017, Microsoft had rolled out a security patch (MS17-010), most organizations didn't install it, putting them at risk.
  • The Backdoor (DoublePulsar): The worm used the EternalBlue exploit to enter, then often inserted the "DoublePulsar" backdoor toolkit in order to inject the WannaCry ransom payload and spread to other vulnerable devices in the network.

3. What was stolen or damaged?

The motive was extortion through withholding of data, not stealing. There is no indication that data was exfiltrated. The most severe harm was operational and financial, with combined losses of up to US$4 billion.

The results was catastrophic:

  • Critical Infrastructure: The National Health Service (NHS) in the United Kingdom was seriously impacted, being forced to delay as many as 19,000 appointments as 70,000 devices, such as computers and MRI scanners, were impacted.
  • Global Corporations: Multinational corporations such as Nissan, Renault, FedEx, and Deutsche Bahn had to suspend their production and operations
  • Data Encryption: The files in hundreds of thousands of computers were made inaccessible, incapacitating businesses as well as government services

    4. How did the world respond?

    May 12, 2017

    The Kill-Switch Discovery

    Hutchins, a researcher, uncovered a "kill-switch" domain that was hard-coded in the malware. By registering the domain, he triggered a function that stopped the spread of the worm, buying the world vital time to upgrade systems.

    May 13, 2017

    Microsoft's Emergency Patches

    In an extraordinary move, the software giant released out-of-cycle security patches against end-of-life software like Windows XP and Windows 8, no longer maintained by the vendor, yet being exploited.

    May 14-22, 2017

    Decryption Tools & Countermeasures

    Experts designed tools such as "WannaKey" and "Wanakiwi" that had the ability to retrieve the encryption keys from memory in specific systems. Experts also urged victims not to pay the ransom, discouraging the attackers' incomes.

    December 18, 2017

    Official Government Attribution

    The U.S. and British governments officially attributed the attack to North Korea. U.S. Homeland Security Advisor, Tom Bossert, claimed the blame was "based on evidence" and not made "lightly."
